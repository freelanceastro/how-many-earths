How many Earths?

Stargazing

Looking up at the night sky, have you ever wondered how many other planets might be circling those pinpricks of light – some maybe populated by sentient beings gazing back at us? Today, thanks to NASA's Kepler space telescope, we're starting to get a handle on the number of roughly Earth-sized worlds that sit within the Goldilocks zone – neither too hot, nor too cold for life to emerge – around their parent stars.

Kepler's haul

Here are the locations of the candidate planets discovered in Kepler's data so far. To estimate the number of alien Earths we must extrapolate from these candidates, based on what astronomers know about the telescope's limits of detection.

Some of Kepler's planet candidates will turn out to be false alarms. Other signals are just be too faint to be seen against the natural variability in a star's glow. As it happens, these two numbers, representing about 10 per cent of Kepler's planet candidates, roughly cancel one another out.

Much more important to our quest is the large number of planets that are missed because the tilt of their orbits means they never pass between their parent stars and Kepler's vantage point, as shown below.

Ripe for life?

Not all of these planets will be rocky worlds like our Earth, with life-sustaining liquid water. In some systems, for instance, carbon-rich planets could form with a crust made from graphite, layers of diamond below, plus rivers and lakes of oils and tar.

Kepler cannot determine a planet's composition, but it can establish whether a roughly Earth-sized planet is orbiting in the habitable zone around its parent star – the range of distances where its surface temperature would allow water to exist in liquid form.

These might be like home

Having removed everything lying outside habitable zones, we're left with our best estimate of the number of potentially life-bearing worlds in the Kepler field of view. The true number will be higher: the telescope failed before it was able to census planets around Sun-like stars with orbits of longer than about 200 days – had Kepler been made by an alien race, looking in our direction, it would have failed to detect the Earth.

What's more, some of the gas giants we excluded earlier could have rocky moons that would be good candidates to host life.

Now consider this: Kepler's field of view covered just 0.28 per cent of the sky. And the telescope was only able to peer up to 3000 light years away, looking just above the plane of our Galaxy.

The bigger picture

As the view rotates, the enormity of the task of scouring the entire sky for habitable worlds becomes clear. Yet this is the mission of Kepler's successor, the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), scheduled for launch in 2017.

Click and drag to tour of TESS's hunting ground, but bear in mind that even TESS will target only some 500,000 of the nearest and brightest stars in its first two years of life. In all, the Milky Way contains as many as 400 billion stars, and it is just one of at least 100 billion galaxies in the Universe.

Viewed from this perspective, the chances that we're alone in the cosmos seem very slim, indeed. Scroll for credits.
