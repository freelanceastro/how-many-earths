HOW MANY
EARTHS?

Looking up at the night sky, have you ever wondered how many other planets might be circling those pinpricks of light – some maybe populated by sentient beings gazing back at us?

Today, thanks to NASA's Kepler space telescope, we're starting to get a handle on the number of roughly Earth-sized worlds that sit within the Goldilocks zone – neither too hot, nor too cold for life to emerge – around their parent stars.

How to spot a planet

Kepler's success in finding distant worlds depends on a conceptually simple method – recording the dip in the glow from their parent stars as the planets pass in front.

In the frame

This is the Kepler field of view, a grid of squares in the northern hemisphere sky. From Kepler's launch in 2009 to the end of its active planet-hunting days in May 2013, the telescope's detectors were trained on some 150,000 target stars in this grid.

Kepler's haul

Here are the candidate planets discovered in Kepler's data so far. Some will turn out to be false alarms. Other planets will be missed because their signals are too faint to be seen against the natural variability in a star's glow.

More important to our quest is the large number of planets that remain invisible because the tilt of their orbits means they never pass between their parent stars and Kepler's vantage point, as shown below.

Planets galore

Adding in these invisible worlds, distributed across Kepler's target stars, the telescope's field of view becomes dense with planets. But many of them will be nothing like our Earth.

Ripe for life?

Not all of the remaining planets will be rocky worlds like our Earth, with life-sustaining liquid water. Kepler cannot determine a planet's composition, but it can establish whether a roughly Earth-sized planet is orbiting in the habitable zone (green, below) around its parent star – the range of distances where its surface temperature would allow water to exist in liquid form.

▼

These might be like home

Here's our best estimate of the number of potentially life-bearing worlds in the Kepler field of view. The true number will be higher: the telescope failed before it was able to census planets around Sun-like stars with orbits of longer than about 200 days.

What's more, some of the gas giants we excluded earlier could have rocky moons that would be good candidates to host life.

Now consider this: Kepler's field of view covered just 0.28 per cent of the sky. And the telescope was only able to peer up to 3000 light years away, looking just above the plane of our Galaxy.

So to estimate the number of potentially habitable planets in the entire Milky Way, we need to extrapolate again.

▼

The bigger picture

Here the Kepler field is zoomed out against an area of sky similar to what you'd see standing on Earth gazing to the heavens. Estimating the number of alien earths in this view, and in the entire Galaxy, is difficult, because astronomers don't know whether Kepler's field of view is typical of the Milky Way as a whole.

But viewed from this perspective, the chances that we're alone in the cosmos seem very slim, indeed.

Scouring the entire sky for habitable worlds is the mission of Kepler's successor, the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), scheduled for launch in 2017.
